β-Blockers in Angina
β₁ blockade → ↓ HR (chronotropy) + ↓ contractility (inotropy) → ↓ MVO₂ + ↑ diastolic filling time → ↑ coronary perfusion time
Atenolol, metoprolol (β₁-selective); also bisoprolol, nebivolol
Propranolol (β₁ + β₂ blockade)
β₁ blockade + stimulates endothelial NO release → vasodilation. Least metabolic side effects, least sexual dysfunction.
Nitrate → ↓ preload + reflex tachycardia; β-blocker → blunts reflex tachycardia + ↓ contractility. Together: synergistic MVO₂ reduction.
Upregulated β-receptors become hypersensitive → rebound tachycardia, ↑ BP, angina, MI. Always TAPER.
Non-selective (propranolol) blocks β₂ → bronchoconstriction. Cardioselective β-blockers (atenolol, metoprolol) are relatively safer but still CAUTIONED at high doses.

Statin MOA & Pharmacology
↓ hepatic cholesterol synthesis → liver senses low cholesterol → compensatory ↑ LDL receptor expression on hepatocytes → ↑ LDL clearance from circulation → ↓ serum LDL
Atorvastatin 40–80 mg, rosuvastatin 20–40 mg → LDL ↓ ≥50%

ADHF & HFpEF
HF Pathophysiology
↑ preload → ↑ SV (up to a point) → plateau/decline. Failing heart operates on a depressed curve.
(1) ↑ SNS: ↑ HR + vasoconstriction; (2) ↑ RAAS: Ang II + aldosterone → Na/water retention; (3) ↑ ADH: water retention
Ventricular hypertrophy, fibrosis (collagen deposition), chamber dilation, cardiomyocyte apoptosis
ACEi/ARBs (↓ Ang II), β-blockers (↓ catecholamine toxicity), MRAs (↓ aldosterone fibrosis), ARNI (↑ natriuretic peptides + ↓ Ang II)
Loop diuretics, digoxin — both relieve symptoms but do NOT reduce mortality in HFrEF
(1) ACEi/ARB or ARNI, (2) β-blocker (carvedilol/metoprolol succinate/bisoprolol ONLY), (3) MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone), (4) SGLT-2i (empagliflozin/dapagliflozin)
Diuretics reduce volume overload and relieve dyspnea/edema (symptom relief) but have NO proven mortality benefit in HFrEF. Do not mistake symptom improvement for disease modification.
ACEi, ARBs, and ARNI
Block ACE → ↓ Ang II → ↓ vasoconstriction + ↓ aldosterone + ↑ bradykinin → ↓ remodeling + vasodilation
Enalapril, lisinopril, captopril
↑ bradykinin (ACE normally degrades bradykinin) → B2 receptor → prostaglandin-mediated cough. Affects 10–15%.
ARB (losartan) — blocks AT1 receptor, NO effect on bradykinin → no cough
Inhibits neprilysin → ↑ ANP/BNP → ↑ cGMP → vasodilation + natriuresis + ↓ fibrosis + ↓ cardiac remodeling
MANDATORY 36-hour washout between ACEi and ARNI. Both ACE and neprilysin degrade bradykinin → dual blockade → massive bradykinin → life-threatening angioedema.
Sacubitril inhibits neprilysin → neprilysin normally degrades BNP → BNP falsely elevated. NT-proBNP not degraded by neprilysin → reliable HF biomarker.
Bilateral RAS, pregnancy (Category D/X), K⁺ >5.5 mEq/L, severe CKD (GFR <30)
MRAs: Spironolactone vs. Eplerenone
Spironolactone: non-selective MRA (also blocks androgen/progesterone) → gynecomastia, impotence, menstrual irregularities. Eplerenone: selective MRA → no sex hormone side effects.
Hyperkalemia — monitor K⁺ closely; avoid if K⁺ >5.0 or GFR <30
NO — dramatically ↑ hyperkalemia and AKI risk without added mortality benefit. Explicitly contraindicated.
Life-threatening angioedema: ACEi blocks bradykinin degradation by ACE; sacubitril blocks degradation by neprilysin. Together → massive bradykinin → angioedema requiring intubation. 36-hour washout is ABSOLUTE.
Switch to eplerenone (selective MRA — no androgen/progesterone receptor binding). Spironolactone blocks androgen receptors → gynecomastia, impotence, loss of libido in men.
β-Blockers in HF
β-blockers are negative inotropes but IMPROVE EF long-term by reversing catecholamine-mediated remodeling (↓ hypertrophy, fibrosis, apoptosis). Long-term benefit outweighs acute negative inotropic effect.
Carvedilol (α₁+β₁+β₂), metoprolol succinate (β₁, extended-release ONLY), bisoprolol (β₁)
ONLY when euvolemic and hemodynamically stable. Start at LOW dose, titrate SLOWLY. Never initiate in acute decompensated HF.
Digoxin
Inhibits Na/K-ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → reverse NCX → ↑ Ca²⁺ → ↑ inotropy (positive). ALSO vagomimetic: ↑ vagal tone → ↓ SA/AV conduction → ↓ HR (rate control in Afib)
0.5–0.9 ng/mL (NOT the old 1.0–2.0 — lower levels equally effective and safer)
(1) GI: N/V/anorexia; (2) CNS: yellow-green visual halos, xanthopsia, confusion; (3) Cardiac: bradycardia, AV block, ANY arrhythmia (PAT with block = classic)
Hypokalemia — K⁺ competes with digoxin at Na/K-ATPase. Low K⁺ → less competition → ↑ digoxin binding → toxicity at 'therapeutic' levels
NO mortality benefit in HFrEF. Reduces symptoms and hospitalizations only (DIG trial).
Digibind (anti-digoxin Fab fragments); also correct hypokalemia; avoid cardioversion in digoxin-toxic arrhythmias
SGLT-2 Inhibitors & Ivabradine
Only drug class proven to reduce HF hospitalizations and mortality in BOTH HFrEF (DAPA-HF, EMPEROR-Reduced) AND HFpEF (EMPEROR-Preserved) — unique!
Empagliflozin (Jardiance), dapagliflozin (Farxiga)
Selectively blocks HCN (If = funny current) channels in SA node → ↓ rate of spontaneous depolarization → ↓ HR only (no effect on contractility, BP, or AV conduction)
HFrEF + HR ≥70 bpm + SINUS RHYTHM + already on maximum tolerated β-blocker dose
NEVER start or up-titrate β-blockers during acute decompensation — heart is dependent on sympathetic support; β-blocker can precipitate cardiogenic shock. Wait until euvolemic and hemodynamically stable.
Furosemide → hypokalemia → less K⁺ competition at Na/K-ATPase → ↑ digoxin binding → toxicity at 'normal' levels. Always monitor K⁺ when both drugs are prescribed.
Ivabradine blocks the funny current (If) in the SA node ONLY. In Afib, the SA node is not driving the ventricular rate — the AV node is. Ivabradine has ZERO effect on ventricular rate in Afib.
Hydralazine / Isosorbide Dinitrate & Diuretics
HFrEF when ACEi/ARB/ARNI cannot be used (bilateral RAS, severe hyperkalemia, CKD, angioedema)
Black patients with HFrEF — A-HeFT trial showed added mortality benefit when added to standard therapy (ACEi + β-blocker)
IV furosemide — rapidly ↓ volume overload, ↓ preload, relieves pulmonary congestion
Blocks NKCC2 in thick ascending limb → ↓ Na/K/Cl reabsorption → massive natriuresis/diuresis; also early venodilation (↓ preload even before diuresis)
Torsemide: oral bioavailability ~80% (more reliable vs. furosemide ~10–90%). Torsemide preferred for outpatient maintenance; furosemide IV preferred in ADHF.
Hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hypomagnesemia, metabolic alkalosis; also hyperuricemia and ototoxicity (high-dose IV)

α-Blockers & Central Sympatholytics
α₁ block → venous pooling → ↓ venous return → ↓ CO → orthostatic hypotension → syncope on first dose. Prevention: start LOW dose at BEDTIME.
α₁A subtype selective (prostate/bladder neck smooth muscle) → less systemic hypotension; primarily treats BPH
Central α₂ agonist → ↓ NE release → ↓ SNS outflow → ↓ HR + ↓ SVR. Abrupt withdrawal → rebound hypertensive crisis (upregulated receptors → massive NE release). ALWAYS taper.
Prodrug → α-methylNE → central α₂ agonist → ↓ SNS. Not teratogenic; decades of safety data. Unique ADR: positive Coombs test (hemolytic anemia), sedation, hepatotoxicity.
Direct arteriolar vasodilator → ↓ SVR → reflex SNS activation (↑ HR) + RAAS activation (Na retention). MUST combine with β-blocker (blocks reflex tachy) + diuretic (prevents fluid retention) = triple therapy.

Warfarin
Inhibits VKOR (vitamin K epoxide reductase) → ↓ γ-carboxylation of factors II, VII, IX, X, protein C, protein S → inactive clotting factors
Factor VII has shortest t½ (~6 hrs) → depleted first → PT/INR ↑. Full anticoagulation requires depletion of factor II (t½ ~60 hrs) → takes 2–5 days.
Non-valvular Afib/DVT/PE: 2.0–3.0. Mechanical heart valves: 2.5–3.5.
4-factor PCC (fastest, most complete) + Vitamin K IV (restores sustained synthesis)
Amiodarone inhibits CYP2C9 → ↑ warfarin levels → ↑ INR → bleeding. Effect persists months (amiodarone t½ ~40–55 days).
Rifampin induces CYP2C9 → ↑ warfarin metabolism → ↓ INR → clotting risk. Need significantly higher warfarin dose.
